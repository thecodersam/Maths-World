Divisibility

1.1: Divisibility Tests

In math, it is very important to know if one number can go into another without remainder. This is called divisibility. Don't understand? Here let me explain what I mean with an animation.

6 / 3 = 2 r 0

6 is divisible by 3 because there is no remainder

10 / 3 = 3 r 1

10 is NOT divisible by 3 because there is a remainder

We'll get to how it is important later, but for now, just know that it is important. But the numbers aren't going to be so easy to calculate all the time. Like, is 3452194 divisible by 3? You could do long division to see, but that can start to get quite time-consuming and annoying as the numbers build up. Luckily, divisibility tests are here to save you. They make it easy to see if something is divisible by 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 and 10. They might take a while to figure out, but once you get the hang of it, you will be seeing if numbers are divisible like a pro.
Remember: You don't always have to use these. Sometimes it's easier to just use long division.

1.2: Factorizations

I need to show you why all this divisibility is important, and it's one, long word, "factorizations". (thinks for a while...) Divisibility, factorizations, man, maths sure has it's long words.

"Factorizing" means "splitting a number into smaller numbers that when multiplied together equals the bigger number." In English, it means finding all the pairs of numbers that when the pairs are multiplied together, they equal the original number. Still not understanding? Let me show you.

Hopefully that gave you a clear image (pun intended) about what factorizing means. Now that you know, let's do an example.

Q: Find the factors of 24

A: So we know 24 goes into 1 because of the '1 divisibility test' and we know it goes 12 times.
It goes into 2 because it's even, and with division, we can see that 2 x 12 = 24.
The sum of 24's digit is 6, so it is divisible by 3, and again, with division we show that 3 x 8 = 24.
It's divisible by 4 and 6 also because of the rules.
So the factors of 24 are 1, 24, 2, 12, 3, 8, 4 and 6.

1.3: Primes and Composite

On the last group of questions, what did you get for no. 1 and 3? Pretty short, right? Just 1 and 11, and 1 and 19. These factors are obvious, because everyone knows that 1 x n = n. Right? So, now I'm going to introduce you to two new words that finally, aren't that long. Prime and composite.

Prime means a number that has only 2 factors, 1 and the number itself. A composite number has more than 2 factors.

Q: Is 44 prime?

A: So we know that this number has factors 1 and 44. We know that if it was prime, it would have only those factors. So, if we know that it has any other factors, we can prove it is not prime.
And we do know! It ends in 4, so 2 is a factor. Now it has more than two factors so 44 is not prime.

That was a short lesson. Now on to exercises!
